Part 1

Sleeping with socks on

a controversial bedtime practice, defended on the merits

The claim

Wearing socks to bed helps you fall asleep faster, stay asleep longer, and may even improve your sex life.

Source: Sleep Foundation, Sleeping With Socks On

The (counterintuitive) mechanism

Warming your feet cools your core.

  • Distal vasodilation: blood vessels in hands and feet open up.
  • Heat radiates out through the extremities.
  • Core temperature drops — which is exactly what your circadian system is trying to do as it ramps up melatonin.

Socks are basically a thermal cheat code for falling asleep.

What the research says

~65–70°F

Optimal ambient temperature for sleep. Socks help your body get there in a cool room.

20 min

Warming the feet for 20 minutes before bed has been shown to reduce insomnia symptoms and fatigue.

The "80% study"

80% vs 50%

In one (much-cited, take-with-salt) study, 80% of couples wearing socks achieved orgasm together, vs. 50% without.

The mechanism is the same: warm feet → comfort → vasodilation. Whether you find this convincing or hilarious is a personal matter.

Other things socks-at-night may help with

  • Hot flashes — cooling the core may reduce night sweats
  • Raynaud's syndrome — prevents cold-triggered flare-ups
  • Cracked heels — traps balm against the skin overnight
  • Generally being a cold person — underrated benefit

Caveats (read this part)

  • Tight or compression socks: no. You want loose-fitting.
  • Circulation problems / foot swelling: talk to a doctor first.
  • Synthetic fibers trap moisture → bacteria → not great.
  • The elderly-insomnia studies didn't show the same benefit. Mileage varies.

How to pick your sleep socks

Material

  • Merino wool (gold standard)
  • Cashmere (luxurious)
  • Cotton (breathable)
  • Avoid: polyester, nylon-heavy blends

Fit & hygiene

  • Loose around the calf
  • No elastic dents in the morning
  • Fresh pair each night
  • Wash feet before putting them on

Verdict

Sleeping with socks on is a cheap, low-risk, evidence-flecked intervention.

If you've never tried it, try it tonight.

Part 2

Bonobos

the pants company, the DTC poster child, the cautionary tale

Origin story

  • 2007. Stanford GSB classmates Brian Spaly and Andy Dunn are tired of men's pants that fit poorly — gap at the waist, baggy through the thigh.
  • Spaly starts sewing prototypes with a curved waistband.
  • They sell them out of a car trunk to classmates. They sell out.
  • The company gets a name: Bonobos.

Why "Bonobos"?

Andy Dunn liked that bonobos (the apes) were "matriarchal, peaceful, and well-dressed by primate standards" — okay, the last part is a joke, but the matriarchal-and-peaceful part is real, and the name was meant to feel friendly and a little weird.

More on the actual primate in Part 3.

The big bet: direct-to-consumer

  • No department stores. No middlemen.
  • Sell pants online, ship them to your door.
  • At the time (2007–2010), this was weird. Men did not buy pants on the internet.
  • Bonobos was one of the very first brands to prove they would.

The clever twist: the Guideshop

  • 2011–2012. Bonobos opens physical "Guideshops."
  • You make an appointment. A guide brings you sizes. You drink a beer.
  • You buy nothing in the store — your order ships from a warehouse.
  • This split — try in store, buy online — became a template that nearly every DTC brand copied.

The money

  • 2007 Founded.
  • 2010 Raises from Accel, Lightspeed.
  • 2013 Spaly leaves; Dunn takes over as CEO. (Spaly later founds Trunk Club.)
  • 2014 Nordstrom invests $16.4M.
  • 2017 Walmart acquires Bonobos for $310M. Peak DTC.
  • 2023 Walmart sells Bonobos to WHP Global + Express for ~$75M. A ~$235M write-down.

What the Bonobos arc actually means

  • DTC, circa 2010, was a real innovation: cut middlemen, own the customer relationship.
  • DTC, circa 2020, was too easy to copy. Everyone had a Shopify and a Facebook ad budget.
  • Customer acquisition costs ate the margins.
  • The Walmart marriage was awkward: a digital-native brand inside an old-economy giant.
  • Bonobos was right about the future. It just didn't get to own it.

Andy Dunn's second act

  • Writes a memoir, Burn Rate (2022), about building Bonobos while managing bipolar disorder.
  • One of the more honest founder books in recent memory.
  • If you read one book about DTC, read this one.

Part 3

The actual bonobo

Pan paniscus · the chillest great ape

The basics

  • One of two species in the genus Pan — the other is the chimpanzee.
  • Share roughly 98.7% of their DNA with humans. About as close as chimps.
  • Found only south of the Congo River, in the Democratic Republic of the Congo.
  • Sometimes still called "pygmy chimpanzees" — a misnomer; they're not actually that much smaller.

A surprisingly recent discovery

  • 1929. German anatomist Ernst Schwarz examines a skull in a Belgian museum that had been labeled "chimpanzee."
  • He realizes it's a separate species.
  • Until 1933, bonobos weren't formally recognized as distinct from chimpanzees.
  • For context: that's after the discovery of insulin, after the Model T, after the first transatlantic flight.

What makes bonobos different

Chimpanzees

  • Male-dominated
  • Inter-group violence is common
  • Resolve tension with aggression
  • Have been observed waging coordinated warfare

Bonobos

  • Female-led coalitions
  • Very little lethal violence
  • Resolve tension with… social bonding
  • "Make love, not war" is the actual scientific shorthand

Why the difference?

  • One leading theory: the Congo River.
  • Bonobos ended up on the south side, where food is abundant and competition with gorillas is low.
  • Less resource scarcity → less reason to fight.
  • Evolution rewarded the apes who got along.

A nice reminder that aggression isn't destiny; it's a response to conditions.

Female power

  • Female bonobos form strong alliances despite not being related — they leave their birth groups as adolescents.
  • These alliances keep individual males from dominating.
  • The result: the highest-ranking individual in a bonobo group is almost always a female.
  • Among great apes, this is unusual. Among mammals generally, it's unusual.

Conservation status

  • Endangered. IUCN Red List.
  • Estimates range from 15,000–50,000 in the wild — we genuinely don't know.
  • Threats: habitat loss, poaching, civil unrest in the DRC.
  • The bonobo's entire wild range is one country, which is both a conservation opportunity and a fragility.

Wrap-up

Three things about socks

Three things

  1. Wear them to bed. Merino, loose, fresh pair.
  2. The company named Bonobos sold curved-waistband pants on the internet, briefly conquered retail, and got bought and re-sold for a $235M loss. Eras end.
  3. The animal named bonobo is matriarchal, peaceful, and runs a better society than most of us. Worth thinking about.
